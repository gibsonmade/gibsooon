# Content Strategy

## Voice

Clear, confident, premium, casual.

Avoid:

- Corporate jargon
- Generic UX language
- Portfolio clichés
- Lorem ipsum
- Unsupported project metrics

## Case Study Voice

Use:

> Gibson helped...

Do not use:

- We helped...
- I helped...

## Messaging Priorities

1. Trust
2. Intelligence
3. Creativity

## Page Roles

- Home: establish positioning, credibility, offer, proof, and inquiry path.
- Work: show flagship case studies and coming-soon proof without overclaiming.
- Case Studies: explain problem, Gibson's role, approach, and result with claims reviewed.
- About: professional profile plus creative magazine feature.
- Resources: small, polished resource hub that does not delay launch.
- Inquire: thoughtful discovery flow for serious-fit projects.

## Case Study Intake Strategy

Use `docs/CASE_STUDY_INTAKE.md` to collect accurate facts before expanding flagship case studies.

Each flagship story needs:

- Publication safety
- Project context
- Gibson's role
- Work performed
- Outcome
- Story angle
- Visual direction

If any fact is uncertain, label it `Needs review` rather than turning it into polished copy.

## Home Depot Story Strategy

Primary angle: Gibson helped The Home Depot make project-based shopping easier by leading UX work around reusable, validated patterns for project configurators and calculators.

What it should prove:

- Gibson can lead product designers and UX researchers inside a large enterprise.
- Gibson can turn complex, high-variation shopping journeys into reusable digital patterns.
- Gibson understands e-commerce, omnichannel retail, design systems, research validation, A/B testing, and cross-functional delivery.

Claims to avoid until approved:

- Specific revenue, conversion, traffic, or statistical results.
- Proprietary screenshots or internal tools.
- Detailed A/B test results.

## CarMax Story Strategy

Primary angle: Gibson helps lead product design for CarMax's sell-your-car journey, a high-traffic supply funnel that helps customers answer the right questions, understand their offer, and choose the right next step.

What it should prove:

- Gibson can own a critical product funnel inside a major consumer brand.
- Gibson can simplify a long, high-stakes form experience with 20+ questions.
- Gibson can use A/B testing, content clarity, offer comprehension, and empathy to improve customer decision-making.
- Gibson can work cross-functionally with developers, financial analysts, product managers, and business partners.
- Gibson is actively applying AI to design and research workflows.

Claims safe to use:

- CarMax hired Gibson directly.
- Gibson started at CarMax in 2021 and is still there in 2026.
- Gibson works on the sell-your-car/supply journey.
- The funnel receives over one million monthly visitors.

Claims to review before publication:

- Specific conversion, revenue, or A/B test results.
- Exact public title phrasing.
- Whether to say "top three product experiences on the website."

## Homepage Hero Strategy

The hero should make the offer explicit:

- Gibson helps founders turn serious ideas into launch-ready digital businesses, brands, websites, and product experiences.
- The offer spans strategy, naming, brand, UX, content, custom visuals, CMS, AI systems, motion, and launch support.
- The page should attract founder-led teams ready to invest in the whole system and quietly filter out tiny one-off production asks.

## Primary Offer Section

End-to-end digital transformation is framed as a three-part arc:

1. Shape: clarify the business idea, audience, offer, name, content, and launch plan.
2. Design: create the brand system, UX, visuals, website direction, and product experience.
3. Build: turn the system into a responsive website, CMS, inquiry flow, and launch-ready foundation.

## Secondary Offers

Secondary offers should read as strategic entry points, not cheap substitutes for the main engagement:

- Product strategy
- UX strategy
- Website strategy
- Digital consulting
- AI and automation consulting

## Fit Filtering

Good fit:

- Founder-led teams with a real business moment.
- Profitable or growing businesses ready to invest in a serious digital project.
- Teams that need strategy, brand, product thinking, and execution connected.

Not the best fit:

- Logo-only projects with no strategy or launch context.
- One-off banners, quick maintenance tasks, or unclear low-budget work.
- Production help where the strategic problem is off limits.

## Inquiry Intro

The inquiry page should make the questionnaire feel selective but not intimidating.

Message:

- The form is for serious founder-led projects.
- The goal is to understand the business moment, scope, budget, and timeline.
- Short answers are fine; specific answers are better.

## Budget, Trust, and CTA Rhythm

- End-to-end engagements should be framed as serious `$10k+` projects.
- Smaller strategy projects can make sense when they clarify a larger launch decision.
- Trust proof should stay concise and specific: enterprise retail, civic creative work, founder launch work, and current AI-driven design.
- The homepage should end with a clear inquiry CTA after proof.
