# Case Study Intake

Use this document to collect only the facts needed to write accurate, publishable flagship case studies.

Rule: do not invent metrics, outcomes, responsibilities, or confidential details. If a detail is uncertain, mark it `Needs review`.

Voice rule: write final case studies as `Gibson helped...`, not `I helped...` or `We helped...`.

## Intake Questions

Answer these for each flagship project.

### 1. Publication Safety

- Can this client be named publicly?
- Were you working independently, through an employer, through an agency, or as part of a larger team?
- Are there any NDAs, confidential details, internal tools, or screenshots that must stay private?
- Are there claims we should avoid?
- Are there approved links, screenshots, press pages, or public references we can use?

### 2. Project Context

- What was the client or organization trying to accomplish?
- What business moment made the project important?
- Who was the audience or user group?
- What was broken, unclear, outdated, or missing before the work started?
- What constraints mattered: timeline, team, budget, platform, compliance, stakeholders, technical debt?

### 3. Gibson's Role

- What was Gibson directly responsible for?
- What did Gibson influence but not own?
- Who else was involved?
- What should the site say to be accurate about collaboration?
- What services apply: strategy, UX, brand, logo, content, custom visuals, website design, website build, CMS, AI systems, motion, launch?

### 4. Work Performed

- What research, discovery, or strategy work happened?
- What concepts, frameworks, flows, wireframes, or prototypes were created?
- What visual design, content, brand, or system work happened?
- What technical implementation work happened?
- What tools or platforms were involved?
- What assets can be shown or recreated safely?

### 5. Outcome

- What changed because of the work?
- Were there measurable outcomes that are safe to publish?
- Were there qualitative outcomes: clarity, speed, confidence, alignment, conversion, usability, launch readiness?
- What did the client/team gain?
- What is the most honest transformation arc: before, during, after?

### 6. Story Angle

- What is the most interesting strategic lesson from the project?
- What should a founder reading this trust Gibson to handle?
- What should this case study quietly prove?
- What would make this story feel specific instead of generic?

### 7. Visual Direction

- Which mood-board row should influence the case study cover?
- What visual assets exist already?
- What needs to be recreated as abstract/safe visuals?
- Are client brand colors allowed as accents?
- Should this story feel more enterprise, civic, startup, luxury, technical, or editorial?

## Home Depot Intake

### Known Draft Title

Helping the Nation's Largest Home Improvement Retailer Sell Projects, Not Just Products

### Answers

- Publication safety:
  - Client can be named publicly.
  - Gibson was hired by The Home Depot as a Senior Manager of UX Design.
  - Date range: 2017-2021.
  - Gibson worked as part of the internal Home Depot organization, leading and managing a team of product designers and UX researchers.
  - Numeric performance claims are not confirmed yet and should be avoided until reviewed.
  - Screenshots, internal tools, test results, or proprietary artifacts need review before use.
- Project context:
  - HomeDepot.com has an enormous product catalog, while home improvement projects are highly customizable and hard for customers to translate into the correct material list.
  - The core customer problem: people often need to make multiple store trips because they do not know exactly what to buy, miss required materials, or choose the wrong items for a project.
  - The team worked on project-based shopping experiences that asked customers for project parameters, then helped generate the right materials list instead of forcing customers to shop item-by-item.
  - Example project areas included countertops, backyard decks, kitchens/cabinets, custom window treatments, custom door treatments, carpet calculators, and drywall calculators.
  - The work needed to support e-commerce and omnichannel shopping: customers could reference a generated list, add it to cart, and choose pickup, delivery, or a combination.
- Gibson's role:
  - Senior Manager of UX Design.
  - Managed and led a team of product designers and UX researchers.
  - Helped spearhead universal, reusable design patterns for project input flows across HomeDepot.com.
  - Ensured designs were validated, tested, followed best practices, and used the Home Depot design system.
  - Coached and grew associates from junior to senior, senior to principal, and supported researchers and designers through mentorship, workshops, guidance, and craft development.
  - Partnered with developers, business owners, analysts, product teams, designers, and researchers to solve problems and deliver projects.
- Work performed:
  - Led pattern creation for reusable project-configuration flows.
  - Helped define what questions to ask, how many questions to ask, appropriate input types, page layout patterns, mobile considerations, and validation approaches.
  - Supported research and testing so project tools were trusted and validated before reuse.
  - Used the Home Depot design system and e-commerce/omnichannel best practices.
  - Helped deliver projects on time and on budget by ensuring the team had the right resourcing and support.
  - Led workshops and internal talks on product design, organizational best practices, and A/B testing.
  - Helped set up meaningful A/B tests for Home Depot products where the organization had enough traffic for significance.
- Outcome:
  - The team contributed reusable, validated UX patterns to the Home Depot digital ecosystem.
  - The work helped shift parts of the shopping experience from individual product browsing toward project-based material selection.
  - Customers could more easily input project needs, select materials, generate a complete product list, and move toward pickup or delivery.
  - The organization gained stronger shared patterns for project calculators/configurators and more consistent UX across teams.
  - Numeric impact claims need review before publication.
- Story angle:
  - The strongest story is not just "enterprise UX"; it is helping a massive retailer sell complete projects instead of isolated products.
  - This case study should prove Gibson can lead teams, create reusable systems, coach designers/researchers, work across large organizations, and translate complex project needs into simpler digital shopping flows.
  - Founder takeaway: Gibson can take a messy, high-variation business problem and turn it into a reusable digital system that helps customers make decisions.
- Visual direction:
  - Use mood-board Row 2 for systems artifacts and Row 4 for case study cover direction.
  - Cover can reference project calculators, material lists, input flows, and retail/product systems without relying on proprietary screenshots.
  - Accent can nod to Home Depot orange, but the page should remain within the black/white/chrome/cosmic editorial system.
  - Visual tone should feel enterprise, systems-led, practical, and high-scale.
- Notes / needs review:
  - Confirm whether any public screenshots or recreated abstract screens are acceptable.
  - Confirm exact years, team size, and whether "Senior Manager of UX Design" should be the published title.
  - Confirm whether A/B testing can be mentioned generally without specific test details.
  - Avoid measurable conversion, revenue, or traffic claims unless Gibson provides approved numbers.

## CarMax Intake

### Known Draft Title

Simplifying How Customers Sell Their Cars Online for the Nation's Largest Automotive Retailer

### Answers

- Publication safety:
  - Client can be named publicly.
  - Gibson was hired directly by CarMax.
  - Date range: started in 2021 and ongoing into 2026.
  - Public website pages and screenshots are acceptable to reference or show.
  - Numeric claim that the funnel receives over one million monthly visitors is safe to say publicly.
  - Avoid unsupported conversion, revenue, or proprietary internal metrics unless later approved.
- Project context:
  - Gibson works on one of the top three product experiences on CarMax.com.
  - The product area is the supply journey: how CarMax buys cars from customers.
  - The journey starts on the selling page on CarMax.com and moves customers through questions, offer delivery, and next steps.
  - The experience needs to convert high-intent visitors into customers who sell their car to CarMax online, in store, or through a hybrid path.
  - The form funnel has over 20 questions, so a major UX challenge is making a long, high-stakes flow feel simple, clear, and trustworthy.
- Gibson's role:
  - Manager of Product Design, equivalent internally to a staff-level design role or similar senior IC/leadership level.
  - Responsible for one of the main funnels on CarMax.com.
  - Helps lead the design of the car-selling journey, including landing page initiation, form-funnel simplification, offer delivery, and next-step clarity.
  - Works cross-functionally with developers, financial analysts, product managers, and other partners.
  - Looked to as a leader in AI, product design, design systems, and testing.
- Work performed:
  - Led design work on the CarMax sell-your-car experience.
  - Ran and shaped extensive A/B testing on the landing page to understand what gets customers to initiate.
  - Tested and improved a form funnel with 20+ questions.
  - Designed offer delivery experiences that help customers understand their offer and decide what to do next.
  - Created and helped spin up zero-to-one products at CarMax.
  - Gave internal talks on AI, product design, design systems, and testing.
  - Used AI in design and research workflows, with the company encouraging experimentation, learning, and teaching others.
- Outcome:
  - Gibson contributed to a high-traffic, business-critical funnel receiving over one million monthly visitors.
  - The work helps customers move from interest in selling a car to an understandable offer and clear next steps.
  - The team improved initiation, form comprehension, and offer understanding through ongoing testing and design iteration.
  - The work supports CarMax's ability to buy cars from customers through a digital and omnichannel experience.
  - Specific conversion, financial, or A/B test results need review before publication.
- Story angle:
  - The strongest story is simplifying a high-stakes, high-traffic decision: helping someone understand the value of their car and decide whether to sell it.
  - This case study should prove Gibson can own critical product funnels, work with complex business logic, design with empathy, and use testing to improve customer behavior.
  - A forward-looking angle: CarMax is also where Gibson sharpened AI-driven design and research practices going into 2026 and 2027.
  - Founder takeaway: Gibson can design high-conversion digital experiences that balance business outcomes, customer trust, content clarity, and cross-functional complexity.
- Visual direction:
  - Use mood-board Row 4 for the case study cover and Row 2 for funnel/system artifacts.
  - Public screenshots are acceptable, but the page can also use abstracted form-flow, offer-card, and testing-system visuals.
  - Accent can nod to CarMax yellow/blue, while staying inside the black/white/chrome/cosmic editorial system.
  - Visual tone should feel high-scale, digital retail, tested, analytical, and human.
- Notes / needs review:
  - Confirm exact published title: "Manager of Product Design" or another CarMax-approved title.
  - Confirm whether "one of the top three product experiences on the website" is safe to publish.
  - Confirm whether the one-million-plus monthly visitors claim should be phrased as "over one million visitors per month" or more generally.
  - Avoid specific A/B test results, conversion rates, revenue, or internal model/process details unless approved.
